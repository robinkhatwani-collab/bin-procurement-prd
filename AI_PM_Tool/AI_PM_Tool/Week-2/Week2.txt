Week 2 - 

TOKENS - 
What is Token - 
A token is the smallest unit of text a model can process — and every cost, every limit, and every architectural decision in AI ultimately comes back to counting tokens. 
In other words - 
A token is not exactly a word. It's a chunk of text — could be a whole word, part of a word, or even just punctuation.

Example - Bank breaks a large payment into smaller processing units. The model breaks text into smaller units it can process.

Real Example
The sentence:
"Authorization was declined"
Gets broken into tokens like:
"Author"  "ization"  " was"  " decl"  "ined"
That's 5 tokens, not 3 words.

Why does the model need tokens  
A model can not read text the way humans do. It only uderstand numbers. 
So the process is: 

Text->Split into Tokens->Each Token get a Number (Token ID) -> Model works with those Number- > Numbers Converted to Text -> Response you read. 

Model never sees the work, it only ever sees the number. 

The Token Vocabulary
Every model has a fixed vocabulary — typically 50,000 to 100,000 tokens. Common words get their own token. Rare words get split into pieces.
TextT okens. Count"
"Visa"  "Visa" 1
"Mastercard" "Master" + "card" 2"


Domain specific jargon costs more token

Context Window - Every model has a context window - The maximum number of tokens it can hold in memory at once. 
Claude Sonnet  →  200,000 tokens  ≈  150,000 words  ≈  ~500 pages
GPT-4          →  128,000 tokens  ≈  ~96,000 words   ≈  ~320 pages

Think of the context window like a whiteboard. Once it's full, early content starts falling off. The model can only reason about what's currently on the whiteboard.
[System Prompt] [Your Documents] [Conversation History] [Your Question] [Answer]
     ↑                ↑                   ↑                    ↑
  ~500 tokens     ~50,000 tokens       ~10,000 tokens       ~200 tokens
  
                    All of this must fit within the context window

Concept - Banking Analogy
Token  - A single transaction entry
Token ID - The transaction reference number
Vocabulary - The bank's master transaction code list
Context Window - The reconciliation batch size limit
Tokenization- Breaking a bulk payment file into individual records


Transformers - 

The Problem Transformers Solved
Before Transformers (pre-2017), models read text one word at a time, left to right — like reading a sentence with your finger covering everything except the current word.
"The transaction was declined because the card was expired"

Old models:  The → transaction → was → declined → because...
             (by the time it reaches "expired" it half-forgot "transaction")
They were slow and forgot context quickly. Terrible for long documents.
Transformers solved this by reading everything at once — and more importantly, figuring out which words relate to which other words, regardless of distance.

The Core Idea — Attention
The breakthrough in Transformers is called the Attention Mechanism.
It answers one question for every token:

"Which other tokens in this text should I pay attention to when understanding this token?"

Simple Example
"The card was declined because it was expired"
When the model processes the word "it" — attention tells it:
"it"  should pay attention to  →  "card"  (90% attention)
                                →  "declined" (7% attention)
                                →  everything else (3%)


How It Actually Works — Step by Step
Let's use a card fraud example:
Input: "Large transaction in Moscow. Card last used in Atlanta 1 hour ago."
Step 1 — Tokenize
"Large" "transaction" "in" "Moscow" "Card" "last" "used" "in" "Atlanta" "1" "hour" "ago"
   ↓          ↓         ↓     ↓       ↓      ↓      ↓     ↓      ↓       ↓     ↓     ↓
  892        4421       287   8833    4201   892    3021   287    7741    16    2019   430
Step 2 — Attention Scores

Every token looks at every other token and asks "how relevant are you to me?"
"Moscow"  is highly related to  →  "Atlanta"   (geography comparison)
"1 hour"  is highly related to  →  "Moscow" + "Atlanta"  (time between locations)
"Large"   is highly related to  →  "transaction"  (describes it)
Step 3 — Build Understanding

After attention, each token is now enriched with context from related tokens. The model now understands:
Not just "Moscow" — but "Moscow as a suspicious location given Atlanta 1 hour ago"
Not just "Large" — but "Large as a risk signal in this geographic context"
Step 4 — Generate Output
"This transaction shows geographic impossibility — high fraud risk"

The Limitation You Must Know
Attention has a cost — every token attends to every other token.
100 tokens   →   10,000 attention calculations   (fast)
1,000 tokens →   1,000,000 calculations          (manageable)
10,000 tokens → 100,000,000 calculations          (expensive)
100,000 tokens → 10,000,000,000 calculations      (very expensive)


This is why context windows have limits — not arbitrary limits, 
but computational ones. And this is exactly why for your card processing agents, you don't want to dump entire rulebooks into the model blindly.

A Transformer reads everything at once, figures out what relates to what, and builds a deep understanding of the whole context — not just the last thing it saw.


Encoder and Decoder - 
Encoder — A deep reader. Sees the whole picture at once. Great at understanding. Outputs meaning, not text.

Decoder — A writer. Builds output one piece at a time. Great at generating. Thinks forward only.

Encoder-Decoder — A translator. Deeply reads one thing, then carefully writes something else based on that understanding.

The Mental Model
Analogy in Card Processing
Encoder - The transaction parser — reads the raw ISO 8583 message, understands every field and their relationships, converts to internal format
Decoder - The authorization engine — takes that understanding and generates a decision + response message
Encoder-Decoder - The full switch — parses incoming message AND generates the response ISO 8583 message back

GROUNDING  
Grounding means anchoring the model's output to verified, specific, external sources — so it cannot freely invent.
WITHOUT GROUNDING          WITH GROUNDING
                           
Model's brain only    →    Model's brain
                           +
                           Your actual Visa rulebook
                           +
                           Your actual transaction data
                           +
                           Your actual policies
                           
Output: plausible         Output: accurate
        but invented              and traceable

Like the difference between asking a consultant from memory versus asking them to answer only from the documents in front of them.

In other words - 
Grounding is the discipline of ensuring the model only uses what you gave it — not what it thinks it remembers — so every decision is accurate, traceable, and defensible.

Grounding Techniques - 
Technique 1 — Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG)
Most common. Fetch relevant facts, inject into prompt.
Question: "What is the dispute timeframe for Visa fraud claims?"

Step 1 — Search your document store
         → Finds page 47 of Visa Core Rules 2024

Step 2 — Inject into prompt
         "Using ONLY this text: [page 47 content]
          Answer: What is the dispute timeframe?"

Step 3 — Model answers from that text only
         "According to Visa Core Rules section 4.7,
          the dispute timeframe is 120 days from 
          transaction date."
Model is now grounded to your document, not its training memory

Technique 2 — Tool Use / Function Calling
Model calls real systems instead of guessing.
Question: "What is the current balance on card 4111 XXXX XXXX 1234?"

Without grounding:
Model guesses or says "I don't know"

With tool use:
Model calls → get_card_balance(card_id="4111...1234")
Real system returns → {"balance": 2847.50, "currency": "USD"}
Model answers → "The current balance is $2,847.50"
The model never invented the number. It fetched it from the real system.
Card Processing Tools You'd Build:
get_transaction_history(card_id, date_range)
check_velocity_rules(merchant_id, amount)
lookup_bin_details(bin_number)
get_scheme_rules(network, rule_type)
validate_cvv(card_id, cvv)

Technique 3 — System Prompt Constraints
Rules baked in before the conversation starts.
SYSTEM PROMPT:
"You are a card fraud analyst assistant.
 Rules you must follow:
 
 1. NEVER give a fraud decision without citing 
    a specific rule or transaction evidence
    
 2. NEVER state a Visa/Mastercard rule from memory —
    only from documents provided to you
    
 3. If you are uncertain, say 'I need to verify this'
    rather than guessing
    
 4. Always end fraud recommendations with:
    'Confidence: High/Medium/Low — Reason: [why]'"
This doesn't eliminate hallucination entirely but constrains the space where the model can invent things.

Train the model on your specific domain data.

RAG 

What is RAG?
Retrieval Augmented Generation.
Three words that tell you exactly what it does:

Retrieval   →   Find the relevant information
Augmented   →   Add it to the model's context
Generation  →   Model generates answer from that information

Without RAG, the model answers from training memory — which is static, potentially outdated, and prone to hallucination.
With RAG, the model answers from your documents, your data, your rules — retrieved fresh every time.

RAG is where all three come together into something you can actually build — a system that retrieves the right document chunks (tokens), understands them deeply (transformer), and answers only from what it found (grounding).

Summary - RAG is the architecture that gives your AI agent a perfect memory — not by training it on everything, but by teaching it to find the right information at the right moment and reason from that alone.

The Problem RAG Solves
Imagine you have:

Visa Core Rules 2024 — 800 pages
Mastercard Transaction Processing Rules — 600 pages
Your bank's internal fraud policies — 200 pages
5 years of chargeback decisions — 50,000 records

You cannot stuff all of that into a context window every time someone asks a question. It would be:
Too expensive    →  Millions of tokens per query
Too slow         →  Model processes all 1,600 pages every time
Too noisy        →  Model gets confused by irrelevant content
Context limit    →  Simply won't fit
RAG solves this by fetching only what is relevant to the specific question being asked.

Deep Dive — Each Step

Step 1 — Chunking
Breaking documents into pieces the model can use
You cannot embed an 800-page document as one piece. You split it into chunks.
VISA CORE RULES 2024 — PAGE 47

"Section 4.7 — Dispute Timeframes
A cardholder must initiate a dispute within 120 days
of the transaction date for fraud-related claims.
For service disputes, the timeframe is 540 days.
Issuers must respond to dispute notifications
within 30 days of receipt..."

                    ↓  CHUNKING  ↓

Chunk 1:
"Section 4.7 — Dispute Timeframes. A cardholder must 
initiate a dispute within 120 days of the transaction 
date for fraud-related claims."

Chunk 2:
"For service disputes, the timeframe is 540 days.
Issuers must respond to dispute notifications
within 30 days of receipt."
Chunking Strategy Matters:
StrategySizeBest ForFixed size512 tokensSimple documentsSentence-basedVariableConversational textParagraph-basedVariablePolicy documentsSemanticVariableTechnical specsHierarchicalParent + childLong rulebooks
For card processing documents:
Paragraph-based chunking works best — Visa and Mastercard rules are written in numbered sections. Each section = one chunk. Preserves logical boundaries.
✓ Good chunk:   One complete rule section
✗ Bad chunk:    Cuts a rule in half
✗ Bad chunk:    Combines 5 unrelated rules

Step 2 — Embedding
Converting text chunks into vectors (numbers)
This is where the Encoder you learned comes in.
Chunk Text
"Cardholder must initiate dispute within 120 days"
         ↓
    ENCODER MODEL
    (e.g. text-embedding-3-large)
         ↓
Vector: [0.82, -0.31, 0.94, 0.17, -0.55, 0.73, ...]
        ←————————— 1,536 numbers ————————————→
The magic: similar meaning = similar vector
"dispute timeframe for fraud"     → [0.82, -0.31, 0.94...]
"how long to file a chargeback"   → [0.81, -0.29, 0.93...]  ← very close!
"authorization approval codes"    → [0.12,  0.67, -0.44...] ← far away
This is why search works semantically — not by keyword matching, but by meaning proximity.
